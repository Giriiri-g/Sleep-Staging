1. title : A CLASSIFICATION OF SLEEP DISORDERS WITH OPTIMAL FEATURES USING MACHINE LEARNING TECHNIQUES,  
Introduction:
  - Describes the increasing prevalence of Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs) globally and in Thailand, highlighting sleep disorders as an early warning sign.
  - Mentions the role of Polysomnography (PSG) in diagnosing sleep disorders, but notes its limitations such as high cost, inconvenience, and the need for trained specialists.
  - Presents the objective of the study: to classify sleep-related syndromes and sleep disorders using machine learning techniques with optimal features.
Related Work
  - Presents an overview of previous research on sleep disorder detection, emphasizing the use of mathematical theories and machine learning techniques.
  - Highlights studies focusing on sleep stage identification, apnea duration effects, ambulatory ECG for OSA detection, neural networks for oxygen desaturation analysis, and mobile phone-based OSA screening.,
Materials and Methods : 
  - Describes the data collection process, including the use of PSG recordings from patients at Songklanagarin Hospital in Thailand.
  - Explains the dataset used, which includes four classes of sleep disorders: Oxygen Desaturation (D), Hypopnea (H), Isolated Limb Movement (I), and Periodic Limb Movement (P).
  - Presents the proposed classification model, consisting of two main steps: data preprocessing and model construction., 
Data Preprocessing : 
  - Explains the data preprocessing steps, including data separation based on sleep stages, normalization of attribute values, and feature selection using Information Gain (IG) with 10-fold cross-validation.
  - Discusses the use of IG to rank features based on their ability to classify data, and presents a table showing the IG values for different features in each sleep stage.
Model Construction
  - Describes the model construction phase, which compares the classification performance of four machine learning techniques: k-Mean Clustering (kMC), k-Nearest Neighbor (kNN), Support Vector Machine (SVM), and Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP).
  - Explains the rationale for selecting these techniques and the specific settings used for each technique.
  - Mentions the use of 10-fold cross-validation to evaluate the performance of the selected machine learning techniques.
Experimental Results
  - Presents the overall classification accuracy results of the selected machine learning techniques in each sleep stage, using graphs and a summary table.
  - Discusses the performance trends observed for each technique, noting that kNN achieved the highest overall accuracy.
  - Analyzes the performance of kMC in detail, explaining its limitations in handling overlapping instances.
  - Highlights the selection of PULSE, SAO2, CANR, and CHEST as optimal features based on their high average IG values and practical considerations., 
Discussion : 
  - Discusses the factors considered in selecting optimal features, including cost, patient comfort, and the need to reduce the number of sensors used.
  - Compares the performance of kNN with different numbers of features, including the proposed opf-kNN model using the four optimal features.
  - Presents the results of paired-sample t-tests, demonstrating that the opf-kNN achieved comparable accuracy to the best-kNN while using fewer features.
  - Discusses the limitations of the study, such as the focus on OSA patients and the use of a single PSG machine.
Conclusion
  - Summarizes the study's findings, emphasizing the identification of optimal features and the development of the opf-kNN technique for sleep disorder classification.
  - Highlights the potential benefits of the proposed model, including its use as a screening test and its ability to optimize bio-signal interpretations for sleep technicians.
  - Notes the study's contribution to determining minimum features required for sleep disorder classification and its potential benefits for hospitals with limited resources.

7. Narcolepsy Diagnosis With Sleep Stage Features Using PSG Recordings
Abstract
  - Describes the difficulty of diagnosing narcolepsy and the need for an objective and accurate method.
  - Mentions the use of polysomnography (PSG) recordings as a gold standard for sleep monitoring and its potential for narcolepsy diagnosis.
  - Highlights the limitations of previous studies that either ignored sleep stage information or relied on manual sleep staging.
  - Presents a novel end-to-end framework for automatic narcolepsy diagnosis using PSG recordings, leveraging sleep staging as an auxiliary task in a multi-task learning approach.
Dataset
  - Describes the collection process of the SSND (sleep-stage narcolepsy diagnosis) dataset, including participant recruitment, PSG recording procedures, and data preprocessing.
  - Presents the statistical analysis of the SSND dataset, including the number of epochs in each sleep stage for patients and normals, highlighting the significant differences in N3 and REM stages.
  - Discusses the hypnogram analysis, comparing examples from a patient and a normal, revealing frequent sleep stage transitions in narcolepsy patients.
  - Analyzes the correlation between different PSG channels using Pearson correlation coefficient, noting high correlation among EEG channels and between chin EMG channels.
Methods
  - Formulates the problem of narcolepsy diagnosis as a mapping function from a sequence of sleep epochs to a narcolepsy prediction and a sequence of predicted sleep stages.
  - Presents an overview of the proposed end-to-end framework, consisting of seven modules: Epoch Feature Extraction Module, Sequence Feature Extraction Module, Sleep Stage Feature Mapping Module, Narcolepsy Feature Mapping Module, Task Feature Fusion Component, Epoch-level Sleep Stage Classifier, and Sequence-level Narcolepsy Classifier.
  - Describes the Epoch Feature Extraction Module, which utilizes CNN, Batch Normalization, and GELU activation function to extract local features within each epoch.
  - Explains the Sequence Feature Extraction Module, employing a Transformer Encoder to capture global context features between epochs in a sleep sequence.
  - Details the Narcolepsy Diagnosis with Sleep Staging Features component, including the auxiliary task of automatic sleep staging, the primary task of narcolepsy diagnosis, and the Task Feature Fusion Component for combining sleep stage features and narcolepsy features.
  - Discusses the joint training process, where sleep staging and narcolepsy diagnosis are trained together using a combined objective function.
Experiment
  - Lists the performance metrics used: ACC (accuracy) and F1-score (F1), with Macro F1 score for sleep staging.
  - Explains the subject-wise 6-fold cross-validation policy adopted for model evaluation.
  - Lists the compared methods: SVM, RF, CNN, CNN+RNN, and Transformer.
  - Presents the overall results, demonstrating the superior performance of the proposed method compared to other approaches on both sleep staging and narcolepsy diagnosis using single-channel EEG (F4-M1).
Analysis and Discussion
  - Analyzes the effectiveness of the auxiliary task of sleep staging by comparing the proposed model with three baseline methods: Single-Task Method, No-Fusion Method, and Two-Phase Method.
  - Discusses the impact of highly correlated channels on model performance, evaluating the use of single channels, highly correlated channel pairs, and all channels for EEG and EMG modalities.
  - Investigates the predictive abilities of different modalities and their combinations for narcolepsy diagnosis, including EEG, EOG, EMG, ECG, Nasal Pressure, and their combinations.
  - Presents a subject-level case study, illustrating the hypnogram and narcolepsy diagnosis results for a selected patient, highlighting the model's ability to correctly score sleep stages and diagnose narcolepsy.
  - Acknowledges the limitations of the study, including the small dataset size, limited subject diversity, and lack of fine-grained narcolepsy categories.
Conclusion
  - Summarizes the proposed end-to-end framework for automatic narcolepsy diagnosis using PSG recordings, emphasizing the use of sleep staging as an auxiliary task and the effectiveness of the multi-task learning approach.
  - Highlights the comprehensive analysis conducted on the PSG recordings, including the importance of sleep staging, the impact of highly correlated channels, and the predictive abilities of different modalities.
  - Mentions future research directions, including addressing the limitations of the current study and exploring the application of the framework to other sleep disorders.
